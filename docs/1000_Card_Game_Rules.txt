1000 (THOUSAND) - CARD GAME RULES - COMPREHENSIVE EDITION
===========================================================

GAME OVERVIEW
=============
1000 (also known as Thousand or Tysiacha) is a classic European card game popular in Eastern Europe,
Poland, and Russia. It is a strategic point-trick game where players aim to be the first to reach 1000 points.
The game combines bidding, trump-suit strategy, and trick-taking mechanics.

PLAYERS: 3 players (fixed)
DECK: 24-card deck (9, 10, J, Q, K, A in each of 4 suits: Hearts, Diamonds, Clubs, Spades)

CARD VALUES IN TRICKS
=====================
Ace (A):     11 points
Ten (10):    10 points
King (K):     4 points
Queen (Q):    3 points
Jack (J):     2 points
Nine (9):     0 points

SPECIAL HAND COMBINATIONS (MARRIAGES)
======================================
A "marriage" is a King and Queen of the same suit held by the same player.
- Announcing a marriage allows declaring the suit as trump and carries strategic significance.
- A player may hold multiple marriages (one per suit) in their hand.
- Marriage points are declared (not scored) and do not contribute to trick points.

SETUP & DEALING
===============
1. Shuffle the 24-card deck thoroughly.
2. Choose a dealer by any agreed method (e.g., highest card or rotating each hand).
3. Deal 7 cards to each player, one card at a time, going clockwise.
   - If 3 players, dealing order: Player 1 → Player 2 → Player 3 → Player 1 → etc.
4. Place the remaining 3 cards face-down in the center of the table (called the "prikup" or "talon").
   - These 3 cards will be given to the winning bidder (the declarer).
5. Game position: Player to the dealer's left is in first position (bids first).

BIDDING PHASE
=============

INITIAL BIDDING
1. The player to the dealer's left bids first (position 1).
2. Bidding starts at 100 points as the minimum bid.
3. Each bid must be:
   - Higher than the previous bid
   - Divisible by 5 (allowed bids: 100, 105, 110, 115, ..., 295, 300)
4. The maximum allowed bid is 300 points.
5. Players bid in clockwise order (position 1 → position 2 → position 3 → position 1, etc.)
6. A player may PASS at any time during bidding. Once a player passes, they cannot bid again in this round.
7. Bidding continues until only one player remains (all others have passed).

DETERMINING THE DECLARER
1. The player with the highest bid becomes the "declarer" (also called "declarant" or "taker").
2. The declarer must score at least the amount they bid to gain positive points for that round.
3. If all players pass on the initial 100-point minimum, the dealer becomes the declarer automatically
   with a bid of exactly 100 points. The dealer can raise this automatic bid.

DECLARER'S OPTIONS AFTER WINNING THE BID
1. Once the declarer wins the bidding, they take the 3 talon cards into their hand.
   - They now have 10 cards total (7 dealt + 3 from talon).
2. The declarer now has two main choices:
   a) SELL THE BID (attempt to pass the contract to another player)
   b) START THE GAME (proceed to play with their current hand)

SELLING THE BID PHASE
=====================

SELLING THE BID - DETAILED PROCESS
1. The declarer may attempt to sell the bid. They do this by selecting 3 cards from their 10-card hand
   and placing them face-up on the table (these replace the talon).
2. The other two players (non-declarers) now get a chance to "buy" the bid by bidding higher than
   the original bid.
   - For example: if original bid was 120, a buyer must bid at least 125.
3. Bidding for the talon proceeds as follows:
   a) Starting with the player to the declarer's left (clockwise), each player may:
      - BID a higher amount (which must be divisible by 5 and higher than current bid)
      - PASS (and cannot bid again in this selling round)
   b) Bidding continues clockwise until only one player remains (the other passed).
   c) That player becomes the new declarer with the higher bid and takes the 3 revealed cards.
   d) The original declarer is EXCLUDED from further bidding on this talon.

SELLING THE BID - OUTCOMES
- If a player buys the bid:
  * They become the new declarer with the new (higher) bid.
  * They take the 3 revealed talon cards into their hand.

- If no one bids on the revealed cards:
  * The revealed cards go back to the original declarer's hand.
  * The original declarer must decide: try to sell again with 3 different cards, or start the game.

SELLING THE BID - MULTIPLE ATTEMPTS
1. The original declarer may attempt to sell the bid up to 3 times per round.
   - Attempt 1: Reveal 3 cards, wait for bids.
   - Attempt 2 (if no buyers on Attempt 1): Put those 3 back, reveal 3 different cards.
   - Attempt 3 (if still no buyers): Put those 3 back, reveal 3 different cards one final time.
2. If no one buys on the 3rd attempt, all revealed cards are returned to the declarer's hand.
3. The declarer MUST now start the game with their 10-card hand. They cannot attempt to sell again.
4. If someone buys the bid on Attempt 1 or 2, the selling phase ends immediately.


STARTING THE GAME PHASE
=======================

CARD EXCHANGE (PASSING CARDS)
1. After the declarer is finalized (either from bidding or from selling the bid), the declarer prepares
   to play by exchanging cards.
2. The declarer selects exactly 2 cards from their hand that they do NOT want to play.
3. These 2 cards are passed face-down to other players:
   - 1 card to the player on their left
   - 1 card to the player on their right

IMPORTANT NOTE ON CARD PASSING
- Players typically pass their weakest or least useful cards.

TRUMP SUIT DECLARATION
======================

TIMING OF TRUMP DECLARATION
1. Trump can only be declared AFTER the first trick has been played (not on trick 1).
2. Trump can only be declared when a player is leading a trick AND holds at least 3 cards in hand
   at the moment they lead (counting the card they are about to play as the lead).
   - Each player has 8 cards after card exchange.
   - When leading trick 7, the player holds only 2 cards; when leading trick 8, only 1.
   - This means trump cannot be declared when leading trick 7 or 8.
3. Trump may be declared when leading any trick from trick 2 through trick 6 (inclusive).

DECLARING TRUMP WITH A MARRIAGE
1. Any player who holds a marriage (a King and Queen of the same suit) may declare that suit
   as trump during the allowed window (after trick 1, while holding at least 3 cards including
   the lead card, and when they lead a trick).
2. To declare trump by marriage:
   a) The player must be leading the trick (playing the first card).
   b) The player plays either the King or Queen of a suit where they hold the marriage.
   c) The player announces the suit as trump (e.g., "Marriage in Hearts" or "Trump Hearts").
   d) That suit becomes the trump suit for all remaining tricks in this round (or a new trump can be declared later).
3. Trump suit effects:
   - Any trump card beats any non-trump card of any other suit.
   - Trump cards win tricks even if they are lower ranking (e.g., a trump 9 beats a non-trump Ace).

NO TRUMP OPTION
1. The declarer may choose NOT to declare any trump suit during the entire round, playing in "no-trump" mode.
2. In no-trump, tricks are won by the highest card of the suit that was led (no trump beats any card).
3. Once all tricks are completed without declaring trump, the round remains no-trump.

PLAY PHASE - TRICKS
===================

STARTING PLAY
1. The declarer leads the first trick (plays the first card).
2. The declarer plays any card from their 10-card hand face-up in the center of the table.
   - NOTE: Only the declarer leads the first trick. After that, whoever wins each trick leads the next trick.

FOLLOWING SUIT (FUNDAMENTAL RULE)
1. The other two players must play in order (clockwise from the declarer).
2. RULE: If a player has a card of the same suit as the card led, they MUST play a card of that suit.
3. EXCEPTION: If a player cannot follow suit, they MUST play trump if they have one (and trump has been declared).
    If they don't have the suit nor trump, they can choose any card they have.

WINNING A TRICK
1. Once all three players have played one card each, the trick is resolved.
2. The winner of the trick is determined by these rules (in priority order):
   a) If trump was played: The highest trump card wins the trick.
   b) If no trump was played: The highest card of the suit led wins the trick.
   c) If a player played a card of a different suit (and no trump), that card does not win.
3. For example:
   - Declarer leads a 10 of Hearts (non-trump).
   - Player 2 plays a King of Hearts (following suit).
   - Player 3 plays a 9 of Spades (trump).
   - RESULT: Player 3 wins the trick (trump beats all non-trump).

   - Declarer leads a 10 of Hearts.
   - Player 2 plays a Queen of Diamonds (cannot follow suit, no trump available).
   - Player 3 plays an Ace of Hearts (following suit).
   - RESULT: Player 3 wins (Ace of Hearts beats 10 of Hearts in the led suit).

COLLECTING TRICKS
1. The winner of each trick collects all three cards played and places them in front of them face down.
2. CRITICAL: The winner of the trick ALWAYS leads the next trick by playing any card from their remaining hand.
   - This applies to all 8 tricks: whoever won the previous trick gets to lead the current trick.
   - The trick leader determines which suit is led (forcing other players to follow suit if possible).
3. Play continues until all cards are exhausted (8 tricks total, since each player started with the appropriate number).

PLAY CONTINUES
1. Each player plays a card in clockwise order for each of the remaining 8 tricks.
2. Players keep their won tricks separate so points can be counted later.
3. No trick is won or lost until all players have played; the highest card (by the rules above) determines the winner.


SCORING PHASE
=============

COUNTING TRICK POINTS
1. After all 8 tricks are played, each player counts the points in their collected tricks.
2. Use the CARD VALUES list at the top of these rules (Ace = 11, Ten = 10, King = 4, Queen = 3, Jack = 2, Nine = 0).
3. A player's trick total is the sum of all card values they won.
4. Example: If a player won tricks containing an Ace, a King, a Jack, and a Nine:
   11 + 4 + 2 + 0 = 17 points

MARRIAGE POINT VALUES
======================
When any player declares a marriage (by playing the King or Queen of a suit where they hold the marriage),
the marriage adds bonus points to that player's score in addition to the card values and trick points:

Clubs marriage:     100 points
Spades marriage:     80 points
Hearts marriage:     60 points
Diamonds marriage:   40 points

Multiple marriages: If a player declares more than one marriage during the round (by leading with K or Q
in different suits), each marriage adds its full bonus value to that player's total score.

DECLARER'S SCORE CALCULATION
1. The declarer's total score for this round is calculated as:
   - Trick points + Declared marriage bonus points
2. Compare the declarer's total to their bid amount:

   a) IF total >= bid amount:
      - The declarer GAINS the bid amount to their total score.
      - Example: Bid was 130, declarer took tricks worth 90 + declared Hearts marriage (60) = 150 total
        → Gain +130 points

   b) IF total < bid amount:
      - The declarer LOSES the bid amount from their total score (negative points).
      - Example: Bid was 140, declarer took tricks worth 85 + no marriage declared = 85 total
        → Lose -140 points
      - A player's score can go NEGATIVE if they fail badly enough.

OPPONENTS' SCORE CALCULATION
1. Each opponent (non-declarer) who did not win the bid scores separately.
2. Each opponent's total score for this round is calculated as:
   - Trick points + Declared marriage bonus points
3. Opponents may declare marriages during play (by playing King or Queen of a suit) to earn marriage bonuses.
   - Use the same MARRIAGE POINT VALUES listed above.
   - Multiple marriages declared by an opponent add their full bonus values.
4. Each opponent gains their total (trick points + marriage bonuses) to their score.
5. Example: Opponent 1 took 23 trick points + declared Spades marriage (80) = 103 total → gains +103 points
6. Example: Opponent 2 took 7 trick points + no marriages declared = 7 total → gains +7 points

ROUND CONCLUSION
1. Each player's score is updated based on the above calculations.
2. The scores for this round are final.
3. A player's cumulative score can go negative — there is no floor. A declarer who loses a large bid
   early in the game may start subsequent rounds with a negative total.
4. Proceed to the next round (next dealer, next bidding phase).

SPECIAL SCORING SITUATIONS
===========================

THE BARREL (880-1000 POINT RANGE) - CRITICAL RULE
1. If a player's score reaches between 880 and 1000 points (inclusive), they are "on the barrel" or "in the barrel."
2. A player on the barrel has a three-round window to reach 1000+:
   - The counter advances by 1 after every round while the player is on the barrel, regardless of outcome.
   - A round where the barrel player does not win the bid (outbid by opponents) still advances the counter.
   - A round where the barrel player wins a bid but fails to score enough also advances the counter
     (and their score drops by the bid amount as normal).
   - These three rounds do NOT need to be consecutive; barrel status persists across rounds until resolved.
3. While on the barrel, the player must bid at least 120. They cannot win the bid at a lower amount.
4. If the barrel player fails to reach 1000 within their three-round window:
   - Their score is reduced by 120 points.
   - They drop out of barrel status and are back below 880.
   - They must then work back up to try again.
5. Barrel resets: If a barrel player successfully wins a bid and scores enough to reach 1000+, they WIN the game.
6. Multiple players on barrel: If multiple players are in the barrel simultaneously, each has their own three-round counter.

EXAMPLE BARREL SCENARIO
- Player A has 895 points (on the barrel, counter = 0 rounds).
- Round 1: Player A bids 130 but only takes 85 trick points. They lose 130 points → 765 total (out of barrel). Counter resets.
- Player A works back up to 890.
- Round 5: Player A bids 140 and takes 150 trick points. They gain 140 points → 1030 total. WINS THE GAME!

- Player B has 920 points (on barrel, counter = 0).
- Rounds 1-3: Player B does not win any bids (other players bid higher). Counter advances to 3 after Round 3.
- After Round 3: Counter reached 3. Barrel penalty applied immediately → 920 - 120 = 800. Player B is now off the barrel.
- Round 4: Player B is no longer on the barrel and bids 110 freely. Takes 85 trick points → loses 110 → 690 total.

THREE CONSECUTIVE ZEROS (ZERO PENALTY RULE)
1. If a single player fails to score ANY points in three consecutive rounds (takes zero tricks or only 9-value tricks),
   they incur an immediate 120-point PENALTY to their score.
2. This penalty is applied immediately after the third consecutive zero round is identified.
3. The counter resets after the penalty, so the player must avoid zeros again in future rounds.
4. This rule discourages complete passivity and ensures participation.

EXAMPLE THREE ZEROS SCENARIO
- Round 1: Player C round points is 0  (counter = 1)
- Round 2: Player C round points is 0  (counter = 2)
- Round 3: Player C round points is 0  (counter = 3)
- PENALTY: Player C loses 120 points immediately. Counter resets.

WINNING THE GAME
================

VICTORY CONDITION
1. The first player to reach a score of 1000 points or more WINS THE GAME immediately.
2. Once a player's total score reaches 1000+, the game ends at the end of that round.
3. If multiple players reach 1000+ in the same round (theoretically rare), the player with the highest score wins.
4. In case of exact tie (both 1000 exactly), the player who reached it first wins (the one in the declarer position).

GAME OVER SCENARIOS
- A player wins by reaching 1000+ points through normal bidding and scoring.
- A player wins from the barrel by successfully scoring 120+ points in a bid and reaching 1000+.

DETAILED ROUND FLOW SUMMARY
============================

A typical complete round proceeds as follows:

1. SETUP: Dealer deals 7 cards to each player. 3 cards placed in talon.
2. BIDDING: Players bid starting from player to dealer's left. Highest bidder becomes declarer.
3. TALON: Declarer takes 3 talon cards (now has 10).
4. SELLING (Optional): Declarer may attempt to sell the bid up to 3 times.
   - If someone buys, they become new declarer with higher bid.
   - If no one buys after 3 attempts, original declarer must play.
5. CARD EXCHANGE: Declarer selects 2 cards to pass to other players.
6. TRUMP DECLARATION: Players can declare trump suit (if they have a marriage) by playing K or Q if they are leading the trick.
7. PLAY: Declarer leads first trick. Players follow suit or play any card. Highest card (per rules) wins trick.
   - The winner of each trick collects the cards and leads the next trick.
   - Continue this sequence for all remaining tricks.
8. EIGHT TRICKS: Repeat trick play 8 times until all cards are played (first trick led by declarer, subsequent tricks led by trick winners).
9. SCORING: Count trick points, apply formulas for declarer and opponents.
10. BARREL CHECK: If any player is now in barrel (880-1000), note their round counter.
11. VICTORY CHECK: If any player has 1000+, they WIN. Otherwise, continue to next round.
12. NEXT ROUND: Move dealer to the left; repeat from SETUP.

MARRIAGE DECLARATIONS (IN-DEPTH)
=================================

WHAT IS A MARRIAGE
1. A marriage is holding both the King and Queen of the same suit in your hand at any point during play.
2. A player may hold up to four marriages (one for each suit), though this is rare in a single hand.

DECLARING A MARRIAGE
1. Any player can declare a marriage during play by leading a trick with either the King or Queen
   of a suit where they hold the marriage.
2. Declaration timing rules:
   - Cannot declare on trick 1 (before any other tricks have been played).
   - Cannot declare when holding fewer than 3 cards (including the lead card) — cannot declare when leading trick 7 or 8.
   - Can declare on tricks 2-6 (inclusive) when leading a trick.
3. When declaring, the player MUST announce the suit and which card (K or Q) they are playing as the lead.
4. Example: "I lead with King of Clubs, marriage in Clubs declared" or "Queen of Hearts, trump Hearts."
   - The marriage is declared by the act of leading with K or Q while holding both cards, plus the announcement.

EFFECT OF MARRIAGE DECLARATION
1. The declared suit becomes the trump suit for next trick (replacing any previous trump).
2. A different player can declare a different suit as trump later in next rounds (by leading with K or Q of a suit where they hold the marriage).
3. The marriage adds bonus points to the declaring player's score (see MARRIAGE POINT VALUES above).
4. Marriage bonuses stack: if a player declares multiple marriages during the round, each adds its full value.

STRATEGIC VALUE
1. A marriage is valuable because:
   - It allows a player to declare trump strategically (at the right moment in play).
   - It provides bonus points when declared.
   - Trump is powerful for winning tricks.
2. Declaring too early in a hand may reveal your strategy to opponents.
3. Waiting until late in the round to declare may limit your ability to use the trump advantage.
4. Without a marriage, you cannot declare trump; the game proceeds in no-trump mode with no opportunity for trump bonus points.

HOLDING A MARRIAGE BUT NOT DECLARING
1. A player may hold a marriage but choose not to declare it during the round.
2. The K and Q are still just regular cards with their face values (4 and 3 points) when played in tricks.
3. If not declared, the marriage provides no bonus points to the player's score.
4. A player might not declare if they prefer to play no-trump or if the timing is not advantageous.

ADVANCED STRATEGIC CONSIDERATIONS
==================================

BIDDING STRATEGY
- Bid conservatively early in the game (avoid big losses).
- Increase bids when holding marriages or high-card combinations.
- Track opponent scores; adjust risk based on their proximity to 1000.
- Never bid more than you're confident you can make.
- If on barrel (880-1000), be more aggressive in the next three rounds.

SELLING STRATEGY
- Sell weak hands to reduce risk of negative points.
- Selling signals weakness; strong players may avoid it.
- Sell to reduce points if you think another player can make a higher bid safely.
- Never sell the exact cards needed to win your bid (keep your best cards).

PLAY STRATEGY
- As declarer, lead your highest-value cards to collect them.
- As an opponent, try to "spoil" the declarer by taking high-value cards in tricks the declarer does not lead.
- Remember which cards have been played to anticipate what remains.
- Preserve trump cards for critical tricks late in the round.

CARD PASSING STRATEGY
- Pass weak cards to opponents to keep your strong cards.
- Some players pass their most dangerous cards (threats to their trick wins).

BARREL STRATEGY
- When on barrel, you have three rounds to make 120+ points. Be aggressive but calculated.
- If you fail, you drop 120 and must work back up. So save risk for when you're confident.
- Opponents may avoid bidding low when you're on barrel (forcing you to be declarer or sit out).

COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID
=========================

1. FORGETTING TO FOLLOW SUIT: Players must follow suit if they can. If they don't and have a suit card, it's a foul.
2. MISCOUNTING TRICK POINTS: Recount the values carefully. An Ace (11) + Ten (10) is NOT 20; it's 21 (11 + 10 = 21).
3. IGNORING THE BARREL: Players forget they're on barrel and bid normally, missing their three-round window.
4. SELLING TOO AGGRESSIVELY: Selling makes you look weak; opponents may bid higher than you could have scored.
5. PLAYING CARDS OUT OF TURN: Each player must wait their turn clockwise. Playing out of turn is a foul.
6. EXPOSING CARDS: Never reveal cards to opponents except as required (marriage declaration, exposed cards).
7. FORGETTING MARRIAGES: Players forget they hold marriages and miss opportunities to declare trump and earn bonus points when leading tricks.
8. MISCALCULATING SCORES: Carefully count trick points and marriage bonuses for accurate score tracking.

EXAMPLE FULL GAME SCENARIO
===========================

SETUP:
- Player A (Dealer), Player B, Player C (to dealer's left, starts bidding)
- Scores: A = 0, B = 0, C = 0

HAND 1:
- Player C bids first. C bids 100, B bids 110, A bids 120, C passes, B passes.
- A (declarer) wins with bid of 120. Takes 3 talon cards.
- A attempts to sell but no one bids. A exchanges 2 cards.
- A has marriage in Hearts. When A leads trick 3, A plays the King of Hearts and declares trump Hearts (60 bonus points).
- Play proceeds: A leads trick 1. Tricks are played. A wins 90 trick points from tricks + 60 marriage bonus = 150 total.
- A made the bid: 150 >= 120, so A gains 120 → A = 120.
- B won 45 trick points → B = 45.
- C won 25 trick points → C = 25.
- No one on barrel yet.

HAND 2:
- Dealer moves to B. C starts bidding again.
- C bids 100, A bids 130, B bids 140, C bids 145, A passes, B passes.
- C (declarer) wins with bid of 145. Takes 3 talon cards.
- C has no marriage. Plays no-trump.
- Play proceeds. C wins only 90 trick points + no marriage = 90 total.
- C failed to make the bid: 90 < 145, so C loses 145 → C = 25 - 145 = -120.
- A won 60 trick points → A = 120 + 60 = 180.
- B won 70 trick points → B = 45 + 70 = 115.
- No one on barrel.

HAND 3:
- Dealer moves to C. A starts bidding.
- A bids 100, B passes, C bids 130, A bids 160, C passes.
- A (declarer) wins with bid of 160. Takes 3 talon cards.
- A has marriage in Clubs. When A leads trick 2, A plays the Queen of Clubs and declares trump Clubs (100 bonus points).
- A exchanges cards. Play proceeds. A's marriage declaration locks Clubs as trump for all remaining tricks.
- A wins 65 trick points from tricks + 100 marriage bonus = 165 total.
- A made the bid: 165 >= 160, so A gains 160 → A = 180 + 160 = 340.
- B won 40 trick points → B = 115 + 40 = 155.
- C won 35 trick points → C = -120 + 35 = -85.
- A gained, B gained, C is negative but recovering.

... (Game continues through more hands)

HAND 25:
- C bids high and wins. C scores 140 trick points, bid was 130.
- C gains 130 → C = 870 (now approaching barrel!)

HAND 26:
- Dealer position rotates. C is in position 2 now (not first to bid).
- A bids 120 and wins. A declares marriage in Diamonds when leading trick 4 (40 bonus points).
- A scores 95 trick points + 40 marriage bonus = 135 total.
- A made the bid: 135 >= 120, so A gains 120 → A = 930 + 120 = 1050 → A WINS THE GAME!

END OF GAME: Player A reaches 1050 points and wins immediately.

GLOSSARY OF TERMS
=================

BARREL: Score range 880-1000 where a player must score 120+ in a bid within three rounds to win.
DECLARER: The player who won the bid and must score at least the bid amount.
MARRIAGE: A King and Queen of the same suit held by the same player.
PRIKUP: The 3 talon cards dealt to the center of the table.
ROSPISAT': Option to pass the round without playing, giving opponents 60 points each.
TALON: The 3 cards in the center dealt from the remaining deck.
TRICK: Three cards (one from each player) played in sequence, won by the highest card per rules.
TRUMP: The suit designated to beat all other suits in trick-taking.
